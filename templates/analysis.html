Forms of Power

Visible

When the power is visible, "the contest over interests is clear in the public space". The visible form of power is also represented by the “formal decisions-making bodies”(political bodies, local assemblies etc)

The assumption behind the visible form of power is that “powerless group can access these decisions-making arenas”. In the sense that “they are able to share their grievance”. In other words, “visible power, assumes that those decisions-making arena are neutral”…. it assumes that the people have the resources and agency to make their voice hear”.

“By seeing who participate, who wins and who loses in these arenas, you can tell who has the power”. “Whose interest prevail?”, “who’s voice is present?”, “whose voice are NOT present?”

Hidden

The hidden form of power is when “certain actors are kept from getting to the decisions-making table - issues are kept off the agenda”. “This is used by certain people in order to maintain their power and their privilege”.

This exclusion is done through : “Dominant rules and procedures”, “How issues are framed”(i.g.: public policy), “the use of threat of sanctions”.

”How people are affected negatively by power”

”Even if hidden, people are able to articulate their grievance”. In other words, they are aware of the fact that they are excluded”

Invisible

The invisible form of power represents “the internalization of powerlessness or lack of awareness”. In other words, “awareness of one’s rights and interests are hidden through the adoption of domination ideologies”

For example people will “internalize the dominant explanation of poverty that tells that poverty is their fault rather than a systemic problem”

Levels of Power

The levels used in the analysis will vary depending on which situation the analysis is applied to. Therefore, the levels have been left out for you to define.

Today, with globalization, more than before the levels of power are “multi-layered and multi-polar - that is, it is found across various levels and among state and non-state actors”. Every layer of power are interacting with each other, “opening and closing opportunities”. “In this approach the vertical dimensions of power should also be seen not as a fixed set of categories, but as a flexible, adaptable continuum”.

Commonly used levels are Sub-National (“decision making arena at this level are critical points of leverage for holding and challenging power” - ex: used by NGO’s), National (“officially represents citizens in global governmental arenas”) and Supra-National (Global governance).

"There are many other levels of power, if you understand them across a vertical continuum from the very micro and personal to the more macro and global. While we have focused on these three levels, spectrum can include family, households, the individual person, organizations, regions, provinces . It can correspond to a country’s administrative structures. It can also reflect places that people experience as being divided along linguistic, ethnic, racial, or political/electoral divides."

(However, the perspective of the influence of globalization may vary. I highly recommend you to read Our Sea of Islands (1994) from Hau’ofa, E. This paper explains that long before what we call globalization, those island nations were trading from island to island and their territory was the land and the ocean. However, once western nations started to define countries and their “worth” in terms of land and GDP, those nations were shrunk and categorized as “poor” in the western globalized world perspective, even though their trade system had been going on for so many years before what we call globalisation and producing wealth - this highlight also the power of the western perspective….)

Spaces of Power

In the Power Cube framework, spaces represent decisions-making arena. It “also includes other spaces that are seen as opportunities, moments and channels where citizens can act to potentially affect policies, discourses, decisions and relationships which affect their lives and interests”.


"Who creates the space is critical to who participates in it." Indeed,"closed space which are being challenged, may seek to restore legitimacy by creating invited space"


Overall, "spaces must be seen like a spectrum" close |---------------| open.


Closed

“Closed spaces are where elites such as politicians, bureaucrats, experts, bosses, managers and leaders make decisions with little broad consultation or involvement”. Note that “closed spaces also exists – and often predominate – in workplace, organization and social movement”!

Invited spaces

In invited spaces people are invited to participate by various kinds of authorities (“government, supra-national agencies or non-governmental organizations”). However, those spaces require new sets of skills for people who never had any opportunities to participate (i.g. expertise on key issue, art of public speaking, negotiating, compromise).

Claimed / Created spaces

Created spaces are “claimed by less powerful actors from or against the power holders”. Andrea Cornwall refers to these spaces as ‘organic’ spaces which emerge ‘out of sets of common concerns or identifications’ and ‘may come into being, as a result of popular mobilization, such as around identity or issue-based concerns, or may consist of spaces in which like-minded people join together in common pursuits".
